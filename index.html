How Physical AI Found Its Voice

I didn’t stumble into Physical AI — I grew into it. Long before the term existed, I was already living at the intersection of the physical world and the digital one. I grew up around systems that had to work — buildings, infrastructure, equipment, people — and I learned early that the real world doesn’t tolerate guesswork. You listen deeply, you understand the full picture, and then you act with precision. That instinct became the foundation of my entire career.

DCIM at Schneider Electric was the spark. That’s where I first saw the truth: the physical world was generating signals, but no one was listening to them properly. No one was translating them into decisions. No one was building the commercial language for what would eventually become Physical AI. So I helped define the DCIM category from scratch — not as “software for data centers,” but as the first real attempt to give physical infrastructure a digital nervous system.

Then came the convergence. At Dell, I reframed cybersecurity as cyber-physical resilience — connecting data protection to uptime, safety, and operational continuity. At Uptake, I bridged OT and IT for predictive maintenance and asset intelligence. At UST, I repositioned capabilities as Physical Operations Intelligence to unlock manufacturing, supply chain, and engineering buyers. Every role was another piece of the same puzzle: how do you commercialize intelligence that lives in the physical world?

Now the world has finally caught up. OT systems, facility management, SCADA, buildings, sensors, Bluetooth labels, frictionless retail — everything is converging with IT. The physical world is becoming computable. And the market is ready for the category I’ve been building toward for 18 years.
